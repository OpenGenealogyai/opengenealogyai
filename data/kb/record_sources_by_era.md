# Record Sources by Era and Location

## United States — 1800s

### Federal Census Records
The US federal census was taken every 10 years starting in 1790. The critical threshold is 1850: censuses before 1850 list only the head of household by name, with other household members counted by age bracket and sex. Starting in 1850, every household member is listed by name, age, sex, and birthplace.

- **1790–1840**: Head of household only. Useful for establishing presence in a location, estimating household composition, finding neighbors (cluster method).
- **1850 and 1860**: First full name censuses. Birthplace is listed at the state level (e.g., "Virginia"), not county.
- **1870**: First census after the Civil War. First census in which African Americans are listed by name throughout the South. Includes column for parents born in foreign country.
- **1880**: Adds relationship to head of household (wife, son, daughter-in-law, boarder). Adds birthplace of father and mother.
- **1890**: Nearly entirely destroyed by fire in 1921. Fragments survive for a handful of states.
- **1900**: Includes month and year of birth, number of years married, number of children born, number of children living, and year of immigration for foreign-born.
- **1910**: Includes whether a survivor of the Union or Confederate Army or Navy.
- **1920**: Includes year of naturalization for immigrants.
- **1930**: Includes age at first marriage, whether a veteran, and war served in.
- **1940**: Includes highest grade of school completed and residence five years prior. Enumerators recorded answers to a long-form questionnaire for every 5th household.

**Access:** Most federal census records are available on Ancestry.com, FamilySearch.org, and MyHeritage. The 72-year privacy rule means 1950 is the most recent publicly available census (released in 2022).

### Vital Records (Birth, Marriage, Death)
Vital registration — systematic government recording of births, marriages, and deaths — began at different times in different states. Most states did not have complete, reliable vital registration until the early 1900s.

- **Early adoption states**: Massachusetts (death registration 1842, birth/marriage 1844), Connecticut and Rhode Island (1840s), New York (statewide 1881, NYC earlier).
- **Late adoption states**: Many Southern states did not achieve complete registration until the 1920s–1930s.
- **Before state registration**: Look for church records, county-level records (some counties required registration decades before states did), family Bibles, and newspaper notices.

Marriage records are often the most accessible pre-registration vital records because marriage licenses and bonds were frequently filed with county courts.

### Land Records
Land records are often the most complete and most underused record type. In the United States, land records fall into two categories:

**Public land states** (most of the US outside the original 13 colonies): Land was originally sold by the federal government through the General Land Office (GLO). Original land entry files are held at the National Archives and are searchable at the BLM GLO Records website. These include the original survey plat, the certificate of purchase, and sometimes correspondence.

**State land states** (the original 13 colonies plus Maine, Vermont, Tennessee, Kentucky, West Virginia, Hawaii, and Texas): Land was distributed by the colonial or state government through patents, grants, and warrants before federal land sales applied.

County deed records are held at the county courthouse (usually the County Recorder or Register of Deeds). They record every subsequent sale, mortgage, and transfer. Deeds frequently name family members, identify neighbors (who may be relatives), and give the seller's previous place of residence — a critical clue for tracing migration.

### Military Records — Civil War
Civil War military records are among the richest sources in American genealogy:

**Service records**: Compiled Military Service Records (CMSRs) exist for both Union and Confederate soldiers. They summarize a soldier's service history from muster rolls and other documents. Available at the National Archives and on Fold3.com.

**Pension files**: Union veterans and their widows could apply for pensions. These files are often enormous — 30–100+ pages — and contain affidavits, medical examinations, marriage certificates, children's names and ages, and testimony from neighbors. They are primary sources for birth dates, marriage dates, and family composition that may not exist anywhere else. Confederate veterans could apply for pensions from their state of residence (not the federal government). State pension files vary by state.

**Regimental histories and muster rolls**: Company muster rolls document when a soldier was present, absent, sick, or detailed to other duty. Descriptive books give physical descriptions (height, eye color, hair color, occupation, birthplace).

**Draft registration records**: Three rounds of Civil War draft registration (1863, 1864, 1865) covered all men 20–45. These cards give name, age, residence, occupation, and physical description.

### Church Records
Before civil registration, churches recorded baptisms, marriages, and burials. The coverage, quality, and survival of church records varies enormously by denomination and region.

- **Catholic parishes**: Maintained registers from the founding of each parish, often in Latin. Diocese archives frequently hold pre-1900 registers.
- **Protestant denominations**: Practices varied. Some denominations (Episcopal, Lutheran, Reformed) maintained parish registers consistently. Others (Baptist, Methodist) were less systematic in early periods.
- **Location**: Records may be held by the individual church, a denominational archive, a state archive, a historical society, or a genealogical society. Many have been microfilmed by the LDS Church (FamilySearch).

## United States — 1700s and Colonial Period

### Overview
The American colonies were settled from roughly 1607 (Jamestown) through the late 1700s. Record survival is dramatically lower than for the 1800s. Many county courthouses burned during the Civil War (Virginia was particularly hard hit), and colonial records were sometimes lost in fires, floods, or simple neglect.

### Colonial Land Grants
Land was the primary unit of colonial wealth. Headright systems in Virginia and other colonies gave land grants to settlers who paid their own passage or the passage of others. These grants often name the grantee and the "headrights" — the people whose transportation was paid.

Patent books, deed books, and land grant records for the colonial period are often held by state archives. Virginia's colonial records are well-preserved in the Library of Virginia.

### Wills and Probate Records
Wills are one of the most genealogically rich colonial records. A will typically names:
- The testator (person making the will)
- Wife and her status (dower rights)
- All children, often with their married names (daughters)
- Grandchildren when adult children predeceased the testator
- Executors (often sons or sons-in-law)
- Witnesses (often neighbors or relatives)

Intestate estates (dying without a will) also generated probate records: inventories, accounts, and letters of administration.

County court records (minute books, order books) contain legal proceedings involving ordinary people and are a largely untapped source.

### Church Vestry Books
In Anglican/Episcopal colonies (Virginia, Maryland, the Carolinas, Georgia), the Church of England vestry handled not just religious life but also poor relief, road maintenance, and vital registration. Vestry books sometimes contain the only surviving records of baptisms, burials, and marriages for a parish.

### Tax Records
Colonial and early national tax records — tithables in Virginia, rateable estates in Pennsylvania — list heads of household and male dependents over a certain age. Gaps in tax records (a man present in one year, absent for several years, then reappearing) can indicate migration, death, or simply tax evasion.

## Immigration Records

### Ellis Island (1892–1957)
Ellis Island was the primary federal immigration station for the Port of New York from January 1, 1892, until its closure in 1954 (final processing 1957). Over 12 million immigrants entered through Ellis Island during this period.

**Ship manifests (passenger lists)** are the primary record type. Manifest formats changed over time:
- **Pre-1906**: Basic information — name, age, sex, marital status, occupation, last residence, destination, amount of money carried.
- **1906 and later**: The expanded manifest (Form 500) added significantly more detail: birthplace, nearest relative in country of origin (name, address, relationship), contact in the US, physical description, and whether the immigrant had been to the US before.

The 1906+ manifests are genealogically valuable because they give the exact town of origin — critical for researching ancestors in foreign records.

**Access:** Ellis Island records are fully searchable and free at EllisIsland.org and FamilySearch. Ancestry.com has indexed versions with images.

**Common errors in indexes:** Names were often recorded phonetically by inspectors who did not speak the immigrant's language. Slavic, Italian, Greek, and other names were frequently anglicized or misspelled. Search by multiple name variations, and always look at the original manifest image rather than relying solely on the index.

### Castle Garden (Before 1892)
Before Ellis Island opened, the primary immigrant processing station for New York was Castle Garden (1855–1890), located at the tip of Manhattan. Records are less complete than Ellis Island but are searchable at CastleGarden.org and through Ancestry.

Before Castle Garden (pre-1855), port records exist for many American ports but are less systematic. The Port of New York has records going back to the 1820s held at the National Archives.

### Naturalization Records
Becoming a citizen was a multi-step process: declaration of intent (first papers, renouncing allegiance to the foreign sovereign) followed by the petition for naturalization (second papers). These could be filed in any court — federal, state, or local. Before 1906, naturalization records are held at the courthouse where the action was taken and are highly variable in content.

After September 26, 1906, the Bureau of Immigration and Naturalization standardized the process. Post-1906 records include detailed personal history, physical description, and information about spouse and children.

Women and minor children were "derived" citizens through a husband's or father's naturalization (until 1922, when women gained independent citizenship).

### Passport Applications
US passport applications (available on Ancestry from 1795) give birthplace, birth date, physical description, and often list accompanying family members. They are useful for confirming the identity of an immigrant and locating the specific town of origin.

## United Kingdom and Ireland

### Civil Registration
**England and Wales**: Mandatory civil registration of births, marriages, and deaths began July 1, 1837. Records are organized by registration district and quarter (Jan–Mar, Apr–Jun, Jul–Sep, Oct–Dec). The GRO (General Register Office) index is searchable online; original certificates must be ordered from the GRO or from the local register office.

**Scotland**: Civil registration began January 1, 1855. Scottish records are notably richer than English records — the original 1855 registers include birthplace of parents, ages and birthplaces of spouses at marriage, and names of both parents on death certificates. Records are searchable at ScotlandsPeople.gov.uk.

**Ireland**: Civil registration began January 1, 1864 (Protestant marriages from 1845). Irish records suffered catastrophic losses in the 1922 Four Courts fire, which destroyed many 19th-century census records. Civil registration records survived and are searchable at IrishGenealogy.ie.

### Census Records (UK)
**England and Wales**: Decennial census records available to the public start in 1841. The 1841 census lists names and approximate ages but not relationships or birthplaces beyond county level. From 1851 onward, the census lists relationship to head of household, exact age, marital status, and birthplace (parish level for most). Records from 1841–1921 are on Ancestry, FindMyPast, and FamilySearch. The 1921 census was released in January 2022.

**Scotland**: Scottish census records (1841–1921) are on ScotlandsPeople and FindMyPast.

**Ireland**: The 1901 and 1911 Irish censuses survive nearly complete and are free online at Census.NationalArchives.ie. Earlier Irish censuses (1821, 1831, 1841, 1851) were largely destroyed.

### Parish Registers (Church of England)
Anglican parish registers begin in 1538 by Thomas Cromwell's order, though few survive from the 16th century. Most surviving registers date from the 17th century onward. Parish registers record baptisms, marriages, and burials — not births, marriages, and deaths. The distinction matters: a child might be baptized weeks or months after birth.

Registers are held at county record offices (archives), often microfilmed by the LDS Church. Many have been transcribed and indexed on Ancestry and FindMyPast.

**Rose's Act (1812)**: Standardized the format of Anglican registers, dramatically improving quality after this date.

**Hardwicke's Marriage Act (1754)**: Required all marriages (except Quaker and Jewish) to take place in the Church of England. This concentrated marriage records in Anglican registers until 1837 civil registration.

### Griffith's Valuation (Ireland)
Griffith's Primary Valuation (1847–1864) surveyed all property in Ireland for tax purposes. It is one of the most important Irish genealogical records because it effectively substitutes for the lost censuses. It lists the name of every occupier of land or buildings, the immediate lessor (landlord), the description of the holding, and the assessed annual value. Searchable free at AskAboutIreland.ie and Askaboutireland.

### Civil Survey and Down Survey (Ireland, 1650s)
The Down Survey and Civil Survey of the 1650s were conducted as part of the Cromwellian confiscations. They provide evidence of landownership and family presence in specific townlands before civil registration and before most parish registers begin.

## Germany

### Church Books (Kirchenbücher)
Before civil registration, German records are found in church books maintained by Lutheran, Catholic, and Reformed parishes. These record baptisms, marriages, and burials (often with cause of death). German church books frequently give the father's occupation, the godparents' names (often relatives), and the mother's maiden name.

**Access**: Many German church books have been microfilmed by the LDS Church and are on FamilySearch. The Archion (archion.de) and Matricula (data.matricula-online.eu) online portals provide access to digitized Catholic and Protestant church books respectively.

### Civil Registration
Civil registration began at different times in different German states:
- **Prussia**: 1874 (some areas earlier due to French influence — Rhine Province from 1798)
- **Bavaria**: 1876
- **Baden, Wurttemberg, Hesse**: 1870
- **Alsace-Lorraine** (French influence): 1792

Civil registration records are held at the local Standesamt (civil registry office) and, for older records, at state archives.

### Emigration Records
German emigration in the 1800s was enormous, particularly 1830s–1880s. Many German states kept emigration lists (Auswandererlisten) recording those who applied for permission to emigrate, which typically required paying off debts and military obligations.

Hamburg emigration lists (1850–1934) are among the most complete in Europe and are searchable on Ancestry and at Ancestry.de. They record the emigrant's name, age, occupation, place of origin, and destination.

### Military Records
Prussian military records are held at the Geheimes Staatsarchiv in Berlin. Muster rolls, substitution records, and discharge papers exist from the 18th century onward. German military records from World War I are partially at the Bundesarchiv-Militärarchiv in Freiburg (many were destroyed in World War II).

## General Online Resources

- **FamilySearch.org** — Free. Largest genealogical database in the world. Billions of records indexed, millions of images. Run by The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints.
- **Ancestry.com** — Subscription. Over 30 billion records. Strong for US, UK, and Central European records.
- **FindMyPast** — Subscription. Strongest for British Isles records.
- **MyHeritage** — Subscription with free tier. Strong for Eastern European records and DNA.
- **Fold3.com** — Subscription (Ancestry company). Specializes in US military records.
- **Newspapers.com / GenealogyBank** — Subscription. Digitized historical newspapers. Obituaries, marriage notices, and legal notices are genealogically invaluable.
- **Google Books / HathiTrust** — Free. Digitized county histories, local histories, and family genealogies that pre-date the internet.
- **National Archives (archives.gov)** — Holds federal records including military service, pension files, naturalization, and census schedules. Online catalog at catalog.archives.gov.
