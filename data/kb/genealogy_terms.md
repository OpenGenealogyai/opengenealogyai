# Genealogy Terms — Reference Glossary

A comprehensive reference of 50+ genealogical terms used in research, records, and the MaxGen standard.

---

## Charts and Documents

**Pedigree chart**
A visual diagram showing direct-line ancestors — parents, grandparents, great-grandparents — radiating outward from a single individual. Also called an ancestor chart. Each person appears once. Does not show siblings, cousins, or collateral relatives. The most common chart in genealogy; the visual representation of a direct ancestral line.

**Family group sheet**
A document recording one nuclear family unit: a couple and their children. Includes birth, marriage, and death dates and places for each person, plus source citations. Unlike a pedigree chart (which shows ancestors), a family group sheet shows siblings. A complete family history consists of a pedigree chart plus one family group sheet per family unit.

**Ahnentafel**
From German: *Ahnen* (ancestors) + *Tafel* (table). A numbering system for direct-line ancestors. The subject (the person being researched) is #1. Their father is #2, their mother is #3. The father's father is #4, the father's mother is #5, and so on. The rule: a person's father is always double their number; their mother is double their number plus 1. An ahnentafel can be written as a numbered list rather than a chart. Also called a "Sosa-Stradonitz" system after the scholars who formalized it.

**GEDCOM**
Genealogical Data Communication — a plain-text file format for storing and exchanging genealogical data. Developed by the LDS Church in 1984. The current widely used version is GEDCOM 5.5.1. GEDCOM files (.ged) can be imported and exported by most genealogy software. Its limitations (poor Unicode support, rigid structure, no standard for evidence quality or confidence scoring) are among the reasons the MaxGen standard was developed.

**Register system**
A numbering system for genealogical reports that follows descendants from a common ancestor. Each new generation of descendants gets a new number series. Used by the New England Historic Genealogical Society's Register (NEHGR) and by most published family genealogies.

## Relationship Terms

**Direct line (lineal)**
The line of ancestry running directly from you to your ancestors: you, your parents, their parents, and so on. Also called the lineal line. Your direct-line ancestors are the individuals who appear on your pedigree chart.

**Collateral line**
Relatives who share a common ancestor with you but are not in your direct line. Your siblings, cousins, aunts, uncles, nieces, and nephews are collateral relatives. Collateral research is often essential for breaking brick walls when direct-line records don't survive.

**Consanguinity**
The state of being related by blood (descent from a common ancestor), as opposed to relationship by marriage (affinity). Canon law and civil law have historically used tables of consanguinity to define prohibited marriage relationships. In genealogical context, consanguinity indicates the degree of biological relationship.

**Collateral descendant**
A descendant of a collateral relative rather than of yourself or your direct ancestors. Your cousin's children are collateral descendants of your shared common ancestor.

**Degree of relationship**
A numerical measure of how closely two people are related. First cousins share one set of grandparents and are said to be related in the second degree (counting back to the common ancestor and down to the other person). Degree calculations differ between civil law, canon law, and common usage.

**Removed (as in "once removed")**
A term describing a generational difference between cousins. Your first cousin's child is your first cousin once removed — you are in the same cousin relationship but one generation apart. "Removed" does not mean estranged or distant emotionally; it means generationally displaced.

**Half-sibling**
A sibling who shares one parent but not both. Half-siblings share approximately 25% of DNA (the same as an aunt/uncle and niece/nephew).

**Step-relationship**
A relationship created by marriage rather than blood. A stepparent is the spouse of one's parent from a subsequent marriage. Steprelationships do not involve biological descent and are indicated in genealogical records by "step-" prefix.

**Affinity**
Relationship by marriage rather than by blood. A person is related by affinity to all blood relatives of their spouse. Affinity is legally significant in some jurisdictions for inheritance and prohibited marriage laws.

**In-law**
A relative by marriage. The term is sometimes used loosely but technically refers only to the direct relatives of one's spouse (father-in-law, mother-in-law, etc.), not to their cousins or more distant relatives.

## Source and Evidence Terms

**Primary source**
A record created at or near the time of the event by a person with firsthand knowledge of the event. A birth certificate signed by the attending physician at the time of birth is a primary source for the birth. Compare: secondary source.

**Secondary source**
A record created after the event, or by a person without firsthand knowledge. A death certificate's information about the deceased's birth date is secondary — reported decades after the fact, usually by a relative who may not know the exact date.

**Original record**
The first recording of information — the physical document, register, or medium in which information was first written down. Distinguished from derivative records.

**Derivative record**
A record created from an original: a transcription, abstract, translation, extract, photograph, or digital image. Derivatives may introduce errors not in the original. Always verify against the original when possible.

**Compiled record**
A record that synthesizes information from multiple original sources. Published genealogies, pedigree files in databases, and family trees are compiled records. Their reliability depends entirely on the quality of the underlying sources and the researcher's methods.

**Abstract**
A condensed summary of a document that preserves the key facts and all names, dates, and places. An abstract does not necessarily quote the document word-for-word. More selective than a transcription.

**Transcription**
A word-for-word copy of a document, including its original spelling, punctuation, and capitalization. Transcriptions should preserve the exact language; errors in the original are transcribed as written and noted with [sic].

**Extract**
Selected portions of a document — specific names, dates, or facts — copied verbatim. Less complete than a full transcription.

**Direct evidence**
Evidence that explicitly answers a research question without requiring inference. A birth certificate that states a birth date is direct evidence for that birth date.

**Indirect evidence**
Evidence that implies an answer to a research question but requires reasoning to connect. A census listing a person as age 32 in 1880 is indirect evidence for a birth year of approximately 1848.

**Negative evidence**
The absence of an expected record or piece of information. If a person does not appear in a census year in a county where they reportedly lived, this absence may be significant — they may have moved, died, or been missed. Negative evidence requires careful interpretation and context.

## Record Types

**Vital record**
An official government record of a life event: birth, marriage, or death. In the US, vital registration is primarily a state function. Vital records are usually primary sources for the event they record and secondary sources for events they report (e.g., birth date on a death certificate).

**Probate**
The legal process of administering a deceased person's estate — proving the validity of a will and distributing assets. Probate records include wills, inventories of personal property, accounts of estate administration, and distribution records. Probate records often name heirs by relationship and give ages and locations, making them valuable genealogical sources.

**Intestate**
Dying without a valid will. When a person dies intestate, the estate passes through probate under the intestacy laws of the jurisdiction. Intestate administration records may be as genealogically valuable as a will, listing heirs and their shares.

**Deed**
A legal document transferring ownership of real property from one person (grantor) to another (grantee). Deeds are recorded at the county level in the US. Deed indexes list both grantor and grantee. Deeds may name family members, give prior residences, and locate neighbors (who may be relatives).

**Land patent / land grant**
The original document by which government transferred public land to a private individual. The first deed in a chain of title. Federal land patents are searchable at the BLM General Land Office Records website.

**Enumerator**
The census-taker who physically visited households to collect census data. Census information is only as accurate as the enumerator's diligence and the household member's willingness to answer correctly. Names were recorded phonetically; ages were often approximated.

**Pension file**
A file created when a veteran or their surviving dependents applied for a pension. Civil War Union pension files are held at the National Archives and can be 50–200+ pages long, containing medical examinations, affidavits, marriage certificates, children's ages, and neighbor testimonies. Among the most genealogically rich records in American research.

**Naturalization record**
A legal record documenting a person's process of becoming a citizen. May include declaration of intent (first papers) and petition for naturalization (second papers). Post-1906 records are standardized and include place and date of birth, physical description, and family information.

## DNA Terms

**Autosomal DNA**
DNA inherited from both parents, across all chromosomes except the sex chromosomes. Used for matching relatives up to about 5th cousins. Tests offered by 23andMe, AncestryDNA, MyHeritage DNA, and FamilyTreeDNA. Autosomal DNA provides the broadest cousin matching but cannot distinguish between maternal and paternal sides without additional analysis.

**Y-DNA**
DNA inherited exclusively through the paternal line — from father to son, unchanged (except for mutations) across generations. Only biological males carry Y-DNA. Used to trace surname lines and identify patrilineal ancestry. The Y-DNA haplogroup reflects ancient paternal origins. Tests offered by FamilyTreeDNA (most specialized).

**mtDNA (mitochondrial DNA)**
DNA inherited exclusively through the maternal line — from mother to all children, but only daughters pass it on. Used to trace matrilineal ancestry across many generations. MtDNA haplogroups reflect ancient maternal origins. Changes very slowly, making it useful for deep ancestry but limited for recent genealogy.

**cM (centiMorgan)**
A unit of measurement for genetic distance. Higher cM values indicate closer relationships. First cousins share approximately 1,700 cM on average; second cousins approximately 230 cM; 3rd cousins approximately 75 cM. The Shared cM Project (DNA Painter) maintains statistical tables of expected cM ranges for various relationship types.

**Haplogroup**
A group of people who share a common ancestor on the patrilineal or matrilineal line, defined by specific mutations in Y-DNA or mtDNA. Haplogroups are labeled with letters and numbers (e.g., R1b, H, J2). They reflect ancient population migrations and are used to trace deep ancestry and ethnic origins. Not the same as recent genealogical ancestry.

**Endogamy**
A cultural practice of marrying within a community or group. Common in Ashkenazi Jewish, Acadian, Amish, and other communities. Endogamy causes DNA matches to appear closer than they are genealogically, because many cousins share multiple lines of common ancestors. DNA matching in endogamous populations requires adjusted interpretations.

**Segment triangulation**
A DNA analysis method that identifies multiple people who share the same DNA segment, implying they share a common ancestor who passed that segment to all of them. Used to confirm genealogical hypotheses and to narrow down which ancestral line a match comes through.

**NPE (Non-Paternity Event)**
Also called "misattributed parentage." A situation where a person's biological father is different from the legal or named father. DNA testing increasingly reveals NPEs in family trees where autosomal DNA matches are inconsistent with expected relationships. Genealogists prefer the term "unexpected parentage result" (UPR) to avoid judgment.

## Geographical and Jurisdictional Terms

**Jurisdiction**
The geographic area under the authority of a specific governmental or legal body. Vital records, court records, land records, and probate records are organized by jurisdiction — usually county or parish in the US and UK. Knowing which jurisdiction your ancestor lived in determines where to look for records.

**County seat**
The city or town where county government is located and where county records (deeds, probate, court records) are held. In the US, the county seat is almost always where courthouse records are physically kept.

**Township**
A subdivision of a county. In US public land survey states, townships are 6-mile-square survey units. In New England, townships are independent municipalities with their own governments. The term means different things in different contexts.

**Precinct**
A subdivision for administrative or electoral purposes. Census enumeration districts and election precincts subdivide counties. In early records, "precinct" sometimes referred to what later became counties.

**Parish**
A church district. In the Southern US colonies, Anglican parishes were the primary civil and religious administrative units. In Catholic Europe, the parish (Pfarrei in German, paroisse in French, parrocchia in Italian) is the unit where vital records were kept before civil registration.

**Hundred**
An English and colonial administrative division between the county and the township or parish. Hundreds were used for tax assessment, militia organization, and court jurisdiction. Still used in Delaware counties.

**Township surveying system**
The Public Land Survey System (PLSS), used in most US states west of Ohio and in parts of the Southeast. Land is described by township and range (e.g., "Township 3 North, Range 5 East, Section 12"). Understanding the PLSS is necessary for reading land descriptions in deeds and land patents.

## Research Standards and Methods

**Genealogical Proof Standard (GPS)**
The professional standard for genealogical research quality, consisting of five elements: reasonably exhaustive search, complete and accurate citations, analysis and correlation of evidence, resolution of conflicting evidence, and a soundly reasoned written conclusion. Meeting the GPS is the difference between documented genealogy and speculation.

**Reasonably exhaustive search**
One of the five GPS elements. Means searching all sources that are reasonably expected to contain relevant information — not just the first confirming source. What counts as "reasonably exhaustive" depends on the time, place, and research question.

**Cluster research (FAN club)**
A genealogical method: researching not just your direct ancestor but their Family, Associates, and Neighbors (FAN). People in historical periods moved in clusters, witnessed each other's documents, and named children after relatives. Following the cluster often breaks brick walls.

**Brick wall**
A colloquial term for a research dead end: a point where no further records can be found for a given ancestor. Brick walls often occur at census years before 1850 (no names before that), at periods of record loss (courthouse fires), at the point where immigration records end, or at the threshold of civil registration.

**Soundex**
A phonetic coding system used by the US Census Bureau to index surnames by how they sound rather than how they are spelled. Each name is coded as one letter followed by three digits (e.g., Washington = W252, Smith = S530). Used to search pre-1940 census indexes where spelling was inconsistent.

**Provenance**
The history of a document or record — where it came from, how it was transmitted, who has held it. Establishing provenance helps evaluate a record's authenticity and reliability.

**Chain of custody**
The documented, unbroken history of who has possessed a document from its creation to the present. Critical for establishing authenticity of physical documents and original records.

**Transcription error**
A mistake made when copying a record — a wrong letter, transposed digits, or missed line. Transcription errors are extremely common in genealogical databases. Always check the original image when an indexed result seems wrong.

**Index**
An alphabetical or numerical list of names in a record set, used to locate specific individuals. Most online genealogical databases are searchable through indexes. Indexes are derivative records and can contain errors; they should always be verified against original images.

**Finding aid**
A guide to the contents of an archive, collection, or record group. Finding aids help researchers understand what records are held in a repository and how they are organized. Critical for researching at archives where records are not digitized.

**Vital record amendment**
A correction to a vital record made after its original filing. Birth certificates may be amended to add a father's name, correct a spelling, or update a gender designation. Amended records may carry an amendment date and may differ significantly from the original filing.
