# Genealogy Research Methodology

## The Fundamental Rule: Start With Yourself and Work Backward

The single most common mistake in genealogy is starting with a famous ancestor and working forward, hoping to connect to yourself. This approach produces unreliable, unverified trees full of errors. The correct method is the opposite: begin with what you know — yourself — and move one generation back at a time, verifying each step before proceeding.

**The backward chain:**
1. You → your parents
2. Your parents → your grandparents
3. Your grandparents → your great-grandparents
4. Continue until records no longer exist

Every link in this chain must be documented with evidence before you extend it further. An unverified link breaks the chain for every generation that follows it.

## The Genealogical Proof Standard (GPS)

The GPS is the professional benchmark for determining whether a genealogical conclusion is sufficiently proven. It has five components:

1. **Reasonably exhaustive search** — You have searched all sources that are reasonably expected to contain information about the person or relationship in question. You have not stopped at the first confirming source.

2. **Complete and accurate citation of each source** — Every piece of evidence must be cited so that someone else could locate the exact record you consulted. Incomplete citations are not acceptable.

3. **Analysis and correlation of the evidence** — You have analyzed each piece of evidence individually and then considered how all pieces relate to one another. Evidence from different sources may confirm, conflict, or be inconclusive.

4. **Resolution of conflicting evidence** — When sources disagree (different birth years, different spellings, different parents), you must explain why you accept one account over another. Ignoring conflicting evidence does not resolve it.

5. **Soundly reasoned, coherently written conclusion** — Your conclusion must be stated in writing with logical reasoning. An unexplained conclusion — even a correct one — does not meet the standard.

Meeting the GPS is the difference between a documented family tree and a speculative one.

## Primary vs. Secondary Sources

**Primary source:** A record created at or near the time of the event by someone with firsthand knowledge.
- A birth certificate created at the time of birth, filed by the attending physician = primary source for the birth date
- A death certificate filed the day someone died = primary source for the death date, but usually a secondary source for birth information (the informant typically was not present at the subject's birth)

**Secondary source:** A record created long after the event, or by someone without firsthand knowledge.
- A death certificate listing the deceased's birth date (reported decades later by a grieving relative) = secondary source for birth
- A county history published in 1890 about settlers who arrived in 1820 = secondary source

**Original record vs. derivative record:**
- An original record is the first recording of information — the actual register, the actual document.
- A derivative record is a transcription, abstract, translation, or digital image of an original. Derivatives may introduce errors not in the original.

Always prefer original records over derivatives. Always prefer primary information over secondary information. When you use a secondary or derivative source, say so explicitly.

## Direct Evidence vs. Indirect Evidence vs. Negative Evidence

**Direct evidence** explicitly answers your research question. A birth certificate that states "born March 4, 1847" is direct evidence for the birth date.

**Indirect evidence** implies an answer but does not state it explicitly. A census record showing a woman as 32 years old in 1880 is indirect evidence that she was born around 1848. You must reason from the evidence to reach the conclusion.

**Negative evidence** is the absence of an expected record. If a man does not appear in any tax records from 1850–1860 for a county where he was supposedly living, that absence may indicate he was not there — or that he was exempt, or that records are missing. Negative evidence requires careful interpretation.

## Handling Conflicting Records

Conflicting records are not a problem — they are a normal part of genealogy research. Most people appear in records where their age is off by a year or two, their birthplace differs between records, or their name is spelled differently in every document. The question is not whether conflicts exist but how you handle them.

**Steps for resolving conflicts:**

1. List all sources and what each says about the disputed fact.
2. Evaluate the quality of each source (original vs. derivative, primary vs. secondary, firsthand vs. secondhand knowledge).
3. Consider context: Why might a person report an incorrect age? Why might a census enumerator write down the wrong information? Was there an incentive to falsify? (Draft evasion, immigration, age requirements for marriage or land ownership.)
4. Weigh the evidence. A vital record filed at birth by an attending physician generally outweighs a self-reported age in a census taken 40 years later.
5. State your conclusion and explain your reasoning. Acknowledge the conflict explicitly rather than hiding it.

If you cannot resolve a conflict with available evidence, say so. "The available sources are inconclusive" is a valid conclusion.

## Brick Wall Strategies

A brick wall is a point in your research where you cannot find the next generation — typically because records don't exist, have not survived, or haven't been digitized.

**Strategy 1: Exhaust all records for the person you know.**
Before declaring a brick wall, make sure you have searched every record type for the known person: census, vital records, land records, probate, church records, military records, newspapers, school records, city directories. Researchers often declare a brick wall after checking only one or two record types.

**Strategy 2: Study the collateral lines.**
Siblings, cousins, aunts, and uncles often appear in the same records as your direct ancestor. A sibling may have left a will naming parents. A cousin's obituary may name the immigrant grandparents. Collateral research frequently breaks brick walls.

**Strategy 3: Work the cluster method.**
Identify all people who appear consistently alongside your ancestor: neighbors in census records, witnesses on deeds, fellow signatories on petitions. These clusters often moved together from a prior location, and following the cluster may lead you to the origin point.

**Strategy 4: Consider name variations.**
Before 1900, spelling was inconsistent. Immigrants often anglicized names. Clerks recorded what they heard, not necessarily what was said. Search for phonetic variations, abbreviations, and nicknames. The Soundex system groups names by phonetic similarity and is useful for census searching.

**Strategy 5: DNA evidence.**
Autosomal DNA matches can break brick walls by identifying cousins who have already traced branches you cannot trace through records. Y-DNA is specifically useful for tracing paternal surname lines. MtDNA traces maternal lines. DNA cannot be destroyed by fire, flood, or institutional negligence.

**Strategy 6: Look at the migration path.**
People moved in patterns — along rivers, along roads, in family groups, from specific origin points. If your ancestor appears in Ohio in 1830 with no prior record, research known migration patterns from that era. Virginians often settled in Ohio and Indiana. Germans concentrated in Pennsylvania, Ohio, and Wisconsin. Knowing where people came from guides record selection.

**Strategy 7: Accept the time boundary.**
Federal census records with names begin in 1850. Civil registration of births/deaths begins in most US states between 1870 and 1910. English civil registration begins in 1837. Irish civil registration begins in 1864. Before these thresholds, you rely on church records, land records, and wills — which are less complete and less indexed. Some brick walls exist because no records survive.

## Research Planning

Never search randomly. Before opening a database, write down:
1. What specific question am I trying to answer?
2. What do I already know about this person?
3. What records would logically contain this information?
4. Where and when was this person living when the event occurred?
5. What is the most likely record type to answer the question in that time and place?

Research without a plan produces unfocused results and wastes time chasing unverified branches.

## Citation Standards

Every source must be cited completely enough for someone else to find the exact same record. A minimum citation includes:
- **Who created the record** (author, agency, institution)
- **What the record is** (record type, collection name, volume/page/certificate number)
- **When it was created** (date or date range)
- **Where it is held** (repository, URL, microfilm number)
- **When you accessed it** (for online sources)

The standard reference for genealogical citation is *Evidence Explained* by Elizabeth Shown Mills. It covers hundreds of source types with specific citation formats.
