Genealogy Has a Truth Problem

Every major genealogy platform forces a single answer. When two records disagree, one gets deleted. When researchers fight over who someone's mother was, the loudest voice wins. The uncertainty — the most honest part of historical research — disappears.

Ancestry / FamilySearch

One answer per fact. Edit wars. Proprietary data you can't export. Your research locked in their system forever.

GEDCOM Standard

40-year-old file format. No confidence scores. No source provenance. Built before AI existed — completely unreadable by modern agents.

OpenGenealogyAI

Every fact carries a confidence score and its source. Multiple possible parents coexist. Nothing is ever deleted. Built from the ground up for AI-powered research.

A Real Example: The Maxwell Family Line

This is a real genealogy problem — the Maxwell family, founder Garlon Maxwell's own ancestors. It shows exactly why confidence scores matter and why "one right answer" isn't how history works.

James Bailey Maxwell (b. ~1870) ← James Bailey Maxwell (b. 30 Oct 1843, Lee Co., IA — d. 16 Jul 1876, Panguitch, UT) ← William Bailey Maxwell (b. 14 Mar 1821, Shawneetown, IL — d. 26 Aug 1895, Mesa, AZ) ← Richard Maxwell (b. ~1796, KY — d. ~Sept 1821, IL) ← Father: Unknown
Well-Documented Link — William's Father
Who is the father of William Bailey Maxwell (b. 14 March 1821, Shawneetown, Gallatin Co., Illinois)?
William was a Mormon pioneer who served in the Mormon Battalion (Company D) and later settled in Mesa, Arizona. He died 26 August 1895. Six independent source systems agree on his father:
Richard Maxwell (b. ~1796, KY; m. Ruth Hodge 20 Apr 1820)
0.90
FamilySearch + WikiTree + Find A Grave + Geni + LDS records + 1959 compiled family history — Richard died ~Sept 1821 when William was only 6 months old; Ruth later remarried William Barnett
Unknown Link — Richard's Father
Who is the father of Richard Maxwell (~1796, likely Bourbon/Pulaski Co., Kentucky)?
The 1959 compiled family history William B. Maxwell and some of his descendants, 1821–1959 — written by direct descendants with access to family Bibles — states explicitly: "The parents of Richard Maxwell are not known." No probate, deed, or Bible record names his father. A GEDCOM forces you to pick one and delete the rest. MaxGen keeps every lead with an honest score:
William Maxwell Jr. (~1775, VA) + Elizabeth Kirkpatrick
0.45
Best geographic fit — William Jr. died in Shawneetown, same town, same year as Richard. Listed in FamilySearch but no primary source proves parentage; zero attached census, deed, or probate
Thomas Maxwell + Elizabeth Wright (m. 1791, Bourbon Co., KY)
0.28
Marriage record found in Bourbon Co., KY — the exact reported birthplace county for Richard, right timing. No children's list survives to confirm or deny
George Maxwell (Bourbon/Madison Co., KY, ~1790s)
0.20
Part of documented Maxwell settler cluster in Bourbon Co. tax lists. Geographic plausibility only — no direct link to Richard found
David D. Maxwell + Mary (Madison Co., KY)
0.08
Large landholder with sons in Madison Co. — adjacent to Bourbon, right era. Speculative; no migration link to Gallatin Co., IL
William Maxwell (~1778?) + Ann Judy
0.07
Appears in scattered Ancestry trees and a Maxwell Y-DNA project alongside a Richard line. Separate from Kirkpatrick line; possible tree error or parallel family
Samuel Maxwell (VA/PA border, ~1776)
<0.05
Active in VA/PA border areas with later western ties — name commonality + broad migration pattern only
Nathaniel Maxwell + Esther Carson (Washington Co., VA)
<0.05
Settled Washington Co., VA ~1770; large family with frontier descendants. No direct KY→IL link documented
Alexander Maxwell (Bourbon Co., KY, ~1790s)
<0.05
Part of Scotch-Irish cluster in Bourbon Co. — speculative; no record ties this Alexander to a son Richard born 1796
John Maxwell Sr. + Margaret Mary Carson (VA/PA → KY)
<0.05
Irish immigrants, multiple sons; descendants pushed to KY frontier ~1770s. No specific record ties them to Gallatin Co., IL by 1820
Every one of these stays open in MaxGen — none is deleted while the research continues. When a researcher finds the Gallatin County probate record, a Maxwell family Bible, or a Y-DNA match in the Maxwell surname project, they update the score and that update applies to every tree that references Richard Maxwell. That is how genealogy should work.

Built on an Open Standard

The data format powering this platform — MaxGen, the Maxwell Genealogy Standard — was built from the ground up so that AI can work and process genealogy data efficiently. Every assertion carries a confidence score, every fact traces back to a source document. You can export your data at any time, build your own tools, and host your own records. The standard belongs to everyone.

MaxGen Record

Every source document stored exactly as found — birth certificates, census rows, parish registers — with its origin and a confidence score attached.

MaxGen Person

A person profile that can hold multiple possible parents at once, each scored by how much evidence supports them. Nothing is ever deleted.

MaxGen Task

A work queue entry for AI agents — search a collection, score candidates, flag conflicts, hand off to a human when needed.

We Need Your GPU

The most important digital library in human history won't build itself. Hundreds of millions of genealogy records exist as scanned images and aging microfilm — birth certificates, census rows, church registers, land patents, immigration papers going back centuries. AI can search them, but only after they are converted into a form AI can understand. That conversion requires GPU power, and it needs to happen at a scale no single organization can achieve alone.

If you have a gaming computer, a workstation, or any machine with a dedicated graphics card, you can be part of this global endeavor — and earn a share of the revenue your compute generates. Contributors who run research tasks for paying users are paid the majority of every dollar collected. The platform keeps a small coordination fee. You do the work. You earn the income.
